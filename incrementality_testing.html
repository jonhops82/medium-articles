Should I be Incrementality Testing my Facebook Ad Spend?

Yes! With the launch of Apple's App Tracking Transparency (ATT), Facebook (and everyone else) now only have the first party data of those iOS devices not opting in. Ie. Facebook platform (conversion) data is not as trustworthy as it once was.

Prior to ATT, Facebook had eyes all over the internet thanks to its Facebook Pixel capturing and sharing back the third party data of all of their advertising partners. So if you had a Facebook Pixel on your website, Facebook could directly tie all conversions which originated from a Facebook click through or view through. Now, Facebook can only see what those ATT devices do on Facebook and they have no way of knowing which of those click throughs and view throughs end up converting. Ie. we have a much worse understanding of how impactful our top of funnel (Facebook) channels are at driving conversions.

In response to ATT, marketers now need a multi-tooled approach, as attribution is now a much more difficult puzzle to solve without the ability to track iOS devices by their unique Identifier For Advertisers (IDFAs). Incrementality testing is just one of three tools I find most useful in understanding attribution in a post iOS 14 world. Note: I have written about the 2 other tools (UTM Tracking, and Media Mix Models) in prior posts.

So what exactly is Incrementality Testing?

Incrementality testing, also known as experimental uplift modeling or causal impact testing, is a statistical method used to measure the causal impact of a marketing campaign, product feature, or any other intervention on a specific business outcome. Here we will focus on its use specifically in marketing.

The goal of incrementality testing is to determine whether a particular intervention (such as a marketing campaign) causes a statistically significant change in the desired outcome, compared to a control group that did not receive the intervention.

To conduct an incrementality test, a randomized controlled experiment is typically used. A random sample of the target population is divided into two groups: the treatment group, which receives the intervention, and the control group, which does not. The outcome is then measured and compared between the two groups to determine whether the intervention had a significant effect.

Is incrementality testing a type of A/B test?

A/B testing is a type of experiment where two or more variants of a web page, app, or marketing campaign are shown to users at random, and the results are compared to determine which variant performs better. A/B tests are often used to optimize user experience, conversion rates, or other metrics.

Incrementality testing, on the other hand, is a technique used to measure the incremental impact of a marketing campaign or other intervention. It aims to answer the question: "did this campaign cause an incremental lift in sales or other business metrics, or would the same outcome have occurred without the campaign?"

While A/B testing can be used as a tool to measure the incremental impact of a campaign, it is not the same as incrementality testing. Incrementality testing is typically more complex and involves more sophisticated statistical techniques, such as regression analysis or propensity score matching, to control for other factors that may have influenced the outcome.

In summary, while both A/B testing and incrementality testing involve testing different versions of a marketing campaign or other intervention, they have different objectives and methodologies.

Where should I use incrementality testing in performance marketing?

The most impactful place to deploy an incrementality testing is optimizing budget allocation. An incrementality test can help you determine which channels or tactics have the greatest incremental impact on business outcomes. By reallocating budgets to the most effective channels, you can maximize the return on investment of your marketing efforts.

More specifically, all marketers should run incrementality testing on Facebook advertising spend. With the release of ATT, the impact of Facebook spend is now very difficult to measure without directly testing it via an incrementality test.

How do I set up an Incrementality Test on Facebook spend?

To set up an incrementality test on Facebook spend, you can follow these general steps:

1. Define your goals: Determine what you want to achieve with your Facebook ad spend. For example, you might want to increase sales, leads, or app installs.

2. Choose your audience: Select a group of people to target with your ads. This group should be randomly divided into two groups with similar historical performance: the test group and the control group. For the most basic test, your audience will be all users.

3. Set up the test: In the control group, your ads will be shown as usual. In the test group, the same ads will be shown, but at a different level of spend. The goal is for the level of spend between the two groups to be significantly different. The most common test is to completely turn off spend in the test group. Thus showing the true difference in performance caused by your Facebook ad spend.

4. Measure the results: After the test is complete, you will need to compare the results between the test and control groups. This will help you determine whether your Facebook ad spend is driving incremental lift, or if it is simply cannibalizing your organic traffic. Keep in mind, conversion data, should come directly from your sales data, NOT Facebook.

5. Analyze the data: Use statistical analysis to determine whether the results are statistically significant. If they are, you can confidently conclude that your Facebook ad spend is driving incremental lift. If not, you may need to adjust your targeting or messaging to achieve better results.

It's worth noting that setting up an incrementality test can be complex, and may require assistance from a Facebook partner or ad agency. However, by following these general steps, you can get started on designing and executing an incrementality test for your Facebook ad spend.

Will incrementality testing improve my Marketing Mix Model (MMM)?

Lastly, incrementality testing will also improve your MMM by providing additional insights into the effectiveness of each marketing channel in driving incremental revenue or conversions. Marketing mix models are analytical tools that help marketers determine how to allocate their marketing budgets across different channels, such as TV, radio, digital, print, and others, based on historical data.

However, MMMs have limitations. The baseline assumption of an MMM is that all marketing channels have the same impact on sales or conversions. And this will continue to be the case if channel spend levels have remained consistent over time. The only way this assumption can be broken is if the historical data has varying levels of ad spend by channel and varying levels of spend distribution across those channels. Thus, incrementality testing can help validate or challenge the baseline assumptions by providing variability in historical spend. This helps the MMM isolate the incremental impact of each marketing channel, thus providing a more accurate understanding of how much each channel contributes to overall revenue or conversions.

By combining the insights from both marketing mix models and incrementality testing, marketers can make more informed decisions about how to allocate their budgets, optimize their campaigns, and maximize their ROI. They can also use these insights to test new marketing strategies or channels, and measure their incremental impact on the business.
