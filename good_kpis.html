Unchecked Assumptions Can Ruin KPIs

Selecting key performance indicators (KPIs) can be a complex process, but there are some general steps you can follow to ensure that you choose the right metrics for your organization or project:

1. Define your goals: Before selecting KPIs, you should have a clear understanding of what you are trying to achieve. This could be increasing sales, improving customer satisfaction, or reducing costs, among other things.

Be careful not to lose sight of the #1 goal, which is likely some measure of revenue, conversions or ROI. A common mistake I have seen is selecting a goal because of an assumed relationship with the real goal. For example, the goal of improving customer satisfaction is ultimately really about increasing revenue, as there is an assumption that an increase in customer satisfaction will cause an increase in retention or LTV. This sounds reasonable enough, but make sure to validate these assumptions and then regularly monitor them, otherwise you're chassing after metrics which don't actually matter.

To play devils advocate with respect to the customer satisfaction metric above: one easy way to improve the customer satisfaction of a subscription product would be to remove auto-renewal, or to give a refund to all renewers who asked for it, regarless of when they ask for the refund. In this example, we are satisfying the goal of increasing customer satisfaction, but at the expense of our real goal, maximizing revenue.

2. Identify metrics that align with your goals: Once you have defined your goals, you should identify metrics that are relevant to them. For example, if your goal is to increase sales, you might consider metrics like revenue growth, conversion rate, or average order value.

Again, be careful not to select metrics with assumed relationships with what you're really trying to measure. Example: A streaming service with an annual subscription model has a #1 goal to increase revenue by X%. They then create a goal to increase rentention by Y% to help acheive their revenue goal. While selecting a metric to track their retention goal, they decide actual retention is a lagging indicator for an annual subscription product, and instead select user engagement.

In this example, the streaming service has made the assumption higher engagement leads to higher retention, a fair assumption. I can say from personal experience that this is not always the case. This may be shocking, but data doesn't lie. I have seen increases in engagement correlated with decreases in retention. If this were the case for the streaming service in our example, they would actually think themselves successful, by increasing engagement, when they may actually be shooting themselves in the foot, by potentially decreasing retention and revenue. So be careful to validate all assumptions before finalizing metrics and continue to regularly monitor them!

3. Consider your audience: KPIs should be understandable and relevant to your audience. Consider who will be using the data and what they will need to know in order to make decisions based on it.

4. Focus on a few key metrics: It's important to select a manageable number of KPIs that provide a holistic view of performance. Too many metrics can be overwhelming and make it difficult to focus on what's important.

5. Ensure metrics are measurable: KPIs should be measurable and quantifiable. This means you should be able to track them over time and use data to analyze trends and performance.

6. Review and adjust regularly: KPIs should be regularly reviewed to ensure they are still relevant and aligned with organizational goals. You may need to adjust them over time as business needs and goals change.

By following these steps, you can select KPIs that provide a clear picture of performance and help you make data-driven decisions to achieve your goals.
